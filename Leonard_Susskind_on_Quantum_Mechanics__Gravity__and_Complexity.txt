2     The 2022 Oppenheimer Lecture: The Quantum Origins of Gravity
2.1    Historical Context and Motivation
In the 2022 Oppenheimer Lecture, Susskind opens with reflections on the sheer astonishment that human beings—
evolved from apes—have deduced the laws of nature to such a high degree of accuracy. He particularly focuses on
how the conflict between quantum mechanics and gravity once appeared intractable. Now, paradoxes and thought
experiments involving black holes suggest that quantum mechanics and gravity are intricately linked, almost as if
two sides of one coin.
2.2    Black Hole Paradox and Holography
Susskind revisits Stephen Hawking’s famous black hole information paradox. In these lectures, he explains how
the paradox led to the holographic principle, which posits that a region of space containing gravity can be mapped
to a lower-dimensional boundary theory without gravity. Susskind highlights that black holes radiate (Hawking
radiation), and that preserving unitarity (the basic principle of quantum mechanics) demands that the black hole’s
interior degrees of freedom must be encoded in subtle correlations of its exterior radiation.
2.3    ER = EPR and the Role of Entanglement
Susskind mentions the ER = EPR idea (joint work with Juan Maldacena) which connects Einstein-Rosen bridges
(wormholes) with quantum entanglement (Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen pairs). He proposes that these two seemingly
disparate concepts might actually describe the same underlying phenomenon. The gist is that an entangled pair
of black holes, or more general quantum systems, can be geometrically interpreted as a wormhole behind their
horizons.

2.4    Summary of Key Points
    • Quantum/Gravity Interconnection: The strong interplay of quantum principles with the curved geom-

    • Black Hole Interiors and Holograms: The interior geometry emerges from subtle quantum information

    • Entanglement as Geometry: Entanglement is not just a calculational tool, but can be interpreted as

3     Complexity and Gravity I
3.1    Overview of Quantum Complexity
Susskind introduces the notion of quantum computational complexity—roughly, the minimum number of simple
gates needed to produce a target state or unitary from a reference (usually the identity). Complexity’s exponential
growth with the number of qubits (K) is crucial:
                                         Maximum complexity ∼ exp(K).
He notes a deep contrast between entropy, which is bounded by K, and complexity, which can grow up to ∼ ecK ,
or in more precise statements, 4K for certain definitions.
3.2    Metrics on Hilbert Space and Complexity as Distance
He discusses two very distinct metrics on Hilbert space (or the space of unitaries):
    1. The Fubini–Study (FS) metric, for which the maximum distance between two states is π/2. This measure
       is familiar but does not capture how “hard” it is to reach one state from another by a sequence of local gates.
    2. The Complexity metric, which treats the minimal gate count as a geometric distance. A pair of states or
       unitaries can be “close” in the FS sense but exponentially far in complexity distance.
Susskind emphasizes that complexity is discontinuous with respect to the usual inner-product metric. Two states
might have almost identical expectation values for all observables yet differ dramatically in their complexity.
3.3    Graph Theory Interpretation
To illustrate complexity growth, one can construct an expander graph where each node represents a unitary, and
each edge corresponds to multiplying by a small (few-qubit) gate from some universal set. Because the unitary
group in high dimensions is extremely large, collisions (two different gate sequences yielding the same final unitary)
are rare up to very large circuit depths, ensuring that the graph outwardly resembles a tree on short time scales.
Eventually, at complexities near 4K , collisions do occur, forming loops in the otherwise tree-like structure. This
sets a maximum complexity for the system.
3.4    Second Law of Quantum Complexity
A central theme is the second law of complexity, an analog of the second law of thermodynamics:
                         d (Complexity)
                                        ≥ 0 (unless complexity is near its maximum).
                               dt
A random Hamiltonian evolution of a state almost always causes the complexity to increase linearly in time, until
saturation (where the system’s complexity is maximal, on the order of 4K ). This draws a parallel with how entropy
tends to increase, but with complexity playing the role of an “entropy” for a higher-dimensional auxiliary space
of gates.

4     Complexity and Gravity II
4.1   Black Holes and the Growth of Wormholes
In the second talk, Susskind connects the second law of complexity directly to the geometry of black holes, par-
ticularly in Anti-de Sitter (AdS) spacetime. He discusses the size of the Einstein–Rosen bridge (ERB) connecting
two entangled black holes (the so-called thermofield double scenario). Using a “Complexity = Volume” (CV) or
sometimes “Complexity = Action” (CA) correspondence, the growth of the black hole interior volume is identified
with the growth of quantum complexity of the boundary state.
4.2   Rindler Time and Bulk-Boundary Dictionary
A direct link is made between the boundary time evolution (in the AdS/CFT sense) and the Rindler time in the
near-horizon region of the black hole. The rate of complexity growth on the boundary is proportional to mass ×
gravitational acceleration, reminiscent of Newton’s laws (F = ma). Indeed, in certain simple models (like the SYK
model or 2D Jackiw-Teitelboim gravity), the rate of complexity growth matches the rate of black hole interior
growth quite precisely.
4.3   Firewalls, Scrambling, and Complexity Plateaus
Susskind addresses the firewall paradox, which stems from the notion that if black holes eventually return their
information (Hawking radiation carrying all bits out), then the interior degrees of freedom might be effectively
“duplicated” outside. The more refined resolution involves scrambling and the idea that once the black hole
becomes sufficiently old, certain naive expectations break down. An old black hole (beyond the Page time in an
evaporating scenario) might still encode interior degrees of freedom in extremely subtle correlations. Complexity
arguments show that forming a firewall corresponds to a period of non-increasing complexity, typically requiring
exponentially fine-tuned states. Hence, a firewall is unstable or extremely rare.
5     Complexity and Gravity III
5.1   Thermodynamics of Complexity and Uncomplexity
In the third talk, Susskind proposes the concept of “uncomplexity” as a valuable resource, akin to negentropy in
classical thermodynamics. When a system’s complexity is far below its maximum, it possesses a store of “uncom-
plexity” that can be tapped to perform computational work. This parallels how a system out of thermodynamic
equilibrium has free energy or negentropy available to do mechanical or computational tasks.
5.2   One Clean Qubit Power
A striking example is the so-called power of one clean qubit, a minimal resource scenario in quantum information
wherein almost all but one qubit are in a maximally mixed state. Surprisingly, this single “clean” (i.e. pure) qubit
can be used, in combination with the rest, to perform nontrivial computational tasks. From a bulk (gravitational)
point of view, the presence of a single qubit of uncomplexity modifies or extends the geometry of the black hole
interior in a calculable way.
5.3   Geometric Interpretation
Finally, Susskind addresses the “Complexity = Action” approach, in which one interprets the action of a Wheeler-
DeWitt patch in the bulk as the complexity of the boundary state. Both the CV and CA proposals highlight how
spacetime geometry emerges from quantum-information theoretic complexity.

6.1    Feynman’s Influence and Intuitive Physics
During the Lex Fridman interview, Susskind reminisces about Richard Feynman’s intuitive style: a method of
closing one’s eyes to visualize the physics first, applying mathematics only once the essential picture is in mind.
Susskind acknowledges that quantum mechanics (and especially advanced topics like entanglement, black hole
interiors, and extra dimensions) remains deeply unintuitive, yet repeated exposure and practice with those concepts
rewires one’s sense of “what is intuitive.”
6.2    String Theory and Large Extra Dimensions
String theory provides a broad framework for unifying quantum field theory and gravity, but Susskind emphasizes
that more than fancy mathematics, it is the holographic principle that has radically reshaped our notion of
spacetime. String theory’s extra dimensions might be physically real but are mostly hidden away in small compact
manifolds (like Calabi–Yau spaces). The broader lesson is that geometry can be emergent from underlying quantum
degrees of freedom.
6.3    Information as Fundamental
Lex and Susskind also discuss whether the universe itself is akin to a giant quantum computer or information pro-
cessing system. Although Susskind concedes it’s a seductive analogy, he is cautious: not every phenomenon is best
viewed as a computer at the fundamental level. Nevertheless, the quantum informational approach (computation,
entanglement, error-correction) has proven extremely powerful for clarifying black hole puzzles and guiding new
insights.
7     Cross-References: Insights from the Two “Three Lectures on Complexity
      and Black Holes” Books
Whenever the three Complexity and Gravity talks are ambiguous, Susskind’s Three Lectures on Complexity
and Black Holes (both the Book and arXiv versions) elaborate further:
    • Definition of Complexity: He rigorously defines relative complexity and the difference between state
      complexity and unitary (operator) complexity.
    • Hamiltonian Evolution and Complexity Growth: Shows that for chaotic or generic Hamiltonians, the
      complexity grows linearly until saturating near the maximum (exponential in K).
    • Thermofield Double & Eternal Black Holes: A two-sided AdS black hole can be described as a
      thermofield double in the boundary CFT. The interior grows with time if the black hole is in equilibrium
      with the boundary. That growth in volume is a direct analog of complexity growth.
    • Exponential Time Scale and Firewall Discussion: After exponentially long times, random recurrences
      or Poincaré recurrences become relevant. But these timescales are so enormous that typical semiclassical
      descriptions break down. Firewalls (or high-energy shockwaves near the horizon) correspond to decreasing
      complexity epochs, which are exponentially improbable.
8     Concluding Remarks
Across these five transcripts, Professor Leonard Susskind presents a cohesive picture:
    1. Quantum & Gravity Intertwined: The quantum mechanical nature of black holes demands a gravita-
       tional theory that is deeply entangled with quantum information.
                                                        5
  2. Holography: All of spacetime within a region can be encoded on a boundary with one fewer dimension.
     This concept originated from black hole thermodynamics and has wide implications (AdS/CFT being the
     prime example).
  3. Complexity as Emergent Geometry: The growth of black hole interiors, wormholes, and the fluid notion
     of “behind the horizon” is intimately tied to the computational complexity of boundary states.
  4. Second Law of Complexity: Just as thermodynamic entropy tends to increase, quantum complexity
     tends to increase under typical (chaotic) Hamiltonian dynamics, only reaching a plateau at exponentially
     large times.
  5. Uncomplexity and Resources: A partially complex (or far-from-maximally-complex) state contains a
     store of “uncomplexity,” analogous to negative entropy or free energy, that can be harnessed for computa-
     tional tasks.
  6. Firewalls and Rarity of Non-Increasing Complexity: States that force complexity to stop growing
     correspond to dramatic phenomena like firewalls. They appear highly fine-tuned and are therefore exceedingly
     rare or short-lived.
   Overall, this synergy of quantum information concepts with black hole and gravitational physics has led to
remarkable insights about spacetime emergence, the resolution of paradoxes, and the deep structure of holography.
