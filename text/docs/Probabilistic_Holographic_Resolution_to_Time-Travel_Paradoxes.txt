A Probabilistic Holographic Resolution to Time-Travel Paradoxes
                                              Jason Agamemnon Sokaris
                                                          Abstract
        We present a single, integrated framework unifying two key constructs that together dissolve classical time-
     travel paradoxes such as the Grandfather Paradox:
        • Faulty Switch Solution: A probabilistic mechanism ensuring that backward-causation signals randomly
          fail or misfire, thus breaking infinite loops and enforcing convergence on a self-consistent timeline.
        • Holographic Memory (HM): A high-dimensional attractor that couples on-shell (physical) states with
          off-shell (virtual/imaginative) states, allowing partial recollection of “erased” or changed pasts, thus solving
          the so-called Memory Problem.
         This approach draws on fields ranging from quantum field theory (off-shell fluctuations) and computational
     complexity (probabilistic rewriting) to game theory (Schelling equilibria), thermodynamics (entropy in branching
     states), and neuroscience (cross-timeline cognition). We add new mathematical formulations here, including the
     multi-loop entropic cost of repeated timeline rewrites and hyperdimensional gradient flows for the Holographic
     Memory. References have been updated for accuracy to the extent possible, and we provide fresh insights on
     how these ideas interlock to make retrocausation logically consistent.

1     Introduction and Background
1.1    Motivation: Paradoxes and the Memory Problem
Time-travel paradoxes famously highlight the apparent contradictions that arise when an agent can alter critical
past events. Deterministic logic either leads to infinite toggles (looping) or demands a strictly self-consistent
timeline. Two especially vexing questions are:
    1. How to break repeated toggles or contradictory loops?
    2. If the past is changed, how can one recall the original timeline?
    Earlier sketches proposed:
    • A Faulty Switch that fails or misfires with small but non-zero probability, breaking the cycle determinis-
      tically.
    • A Holographic Memory that merges real (on-shell) and virtual (off-shell) processes in a high-dimensional
      attractor, preserving partial traces of overwritten histories.
Merging these yields a cohesive and probabilistic resolution to paradoxes, while allowing faint memory across
changed timelines.
1.2    Key Fields and Relevance
Physics (On-/Off-Shell Signals). Quantum field theory distinguishes on-shell processes obeying energy–
momentum constraints from off-shell (virtual) processes that do not. These off-shell fluctuations can provide a
genuine randomness source for the faulty switch and can also ground the concept of a hidden (imagined) domain
for memory.
Computational Complexity. Repeated attempts to alter the past can be viewed as a rewriting system, where
the branching factors correspond to success/failure or spurious triggers. A purely deterministic system may never
halt on a final consistent timeline, but introducing randomness ensures that, with high probability, a stable
configuration emerges. This resonates with concepts in BPP (probabilistic polynomial time) or BQP (quantum
polynomial time).
Thermodynamics and Entropy. Each timeline rewrite can be seen as injecting small random bits, increasing
an “entropy” of possible states. Over repeated loops, the system irreversibly transitions into a stable attractor,
akin to how physical systems move toward equilibrium.
Chaos Theory and Non-Linear Attractors. Small random perturbations in a chaotic system typically pre-
vent stable infinite loops, driving the system toward strange attractors. The Holographic Memory can be viewed
as a high-dimensional attractor that merges contradictory states into a single global manifold.
Game Theory and Schelling Points. One can treat each iteration or timeline as an “agent” attempting to
revert or maintain a state. Without randomness, infinite toggles or contradictory equilibria may ensue. With the
Faulty Switch, the system converges stochastically on a consistent solution (analogous to a Schelling equilibrium).
Neuroscience and Cognition. A cross-time neural dynamic can embed ephemeral or “erased” states within
an off-shell portion of cognition. This explains partial recollections or deja vu of events that are no longer part of
the “main” timeline.

When a timeline is changed, how does an agent recall or compare the old timeline? Purely local memory would
strictly track the final timeline, erasing all references to events undone. Yet anecdotal or fictional accounts often
mention partial recollections (“residual memories”) of undone events.

4     Integration: Faulty Switch + Holographic Memory
4.1   Loop Avoidance and Partial Recall
By incorporating the Faulty Switch (Section 2), we ensure no infinite toggles remain. Meanwhile, the Holo-
graphic Memory framework (Section 3) posits that altered histories remain partially within ψ. Thus, (1) we
avoid paradoxical loops, (2) we keep partial references to undone events.
4.2   Game-Theoretic Convergence and Attractor Stability
One may treat the rewriting of timelines as iterative moves in a multi-agent game. Randomness (from the faulty
switch) ensures the system transitions to an absorbing state. Simultaneously, the hybrid attractor evolves via
Eq. (2), stabilizing into a final (x, ψ) that merges the updated timeline with partial echoes of the old. Hence, the
overall system yields consistent retrocausality without erasing all knowledge of alternatives.

Closed Timelike Curves. This approach can embed into exotic spacetimes admitting closed timelike loops.
Randomness ensures logical consistency, while the hybrid memory ensures no complete oblivion of undone events.
Quantum Computation & FTL Cognition. Off-shell resources (like partial entanglement) might allow ad-
vanced manipulations reminiscent of “superpositions” of old timelines. The synergy with the faulty switch extends
quantum randomness into classical loops.
Neuroscience and Dream Logic. In a future technology, “dream states” might represent the off-shell portion
of cognition, bridging the old and new timelines. The random toggles stop indefinite replays (like nightmares
repeating forever).
7     Conclusion
We have consolidated multiple drafts, expansions, and errant fragments into one cohesive treatise:
    1. Faulty Switch Solution: A minimal injection of randomness into backward-causation signals to break
       infinite loops and contradictory toggles.
    2. Holographic Memory Framework: A higher-dimensional attractor across on-shell (real) and off-shell
       (imagined) states, retaining partial knowledge of overwritten timelines.
    By merging quantum-fluctuation-driven noise and a multi-branch memory architecture, time-travel paradoxes
become statistically rather than strictly avoided, while the agent’s consciousness can hold echoes of changed
histories. This satisfies fundamental consistency demands in a wide interdisciplinary sense—from physics and
chaos theory to computational complexity, game theory, and neuroscience.
